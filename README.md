# NLP Ticket Clustering

Unsupervised topic discovery for customer support tickets using sentence embeddings, UMAP dimensionality reduction, and K-Means clustering.
## Results

| Metric | K-Means (k=22) | DBSCAN (eps=0.7) |
|---|---|---|
| Silhouette | 0.889 | 0.806 |
| Davies-Bouldin | 0.151 | 0.243 |
| ARI | 0.585 | 0.594 |
| NMI | 0.863 | 0.823 |
| Clusters | 22 | 69 |
| Noise points | 0 | 0 |

K-Means recovered **86.3% of customer intent structure** (NMI=0.863) across 4,935 tickets without access to any labels during training.

> K-Means and DBSCAN were evaluated on their own independently optimised UMAP spaces. See [Methodology](#methodology) for details.

## Methodology

### Dataset

**Bitext Customer Support LLM Chatbot Training Dataset** — 26,872 synthetic utterances across 27 intents and 10 categories. Available on HuggingFace.

5,000 tickets were randomly sampled (`random_state=42`) for balanced intent coverage. After preprocessing, 4,935 tickets were retained.

### Preprocessing

**Short ticket removal:** Tickets under 3 words were dropped (46 tickets). Single-word tickets produce embeddings with insufficient sentence context.

**Placeholder entity handling:** The dataset contains unfilled template placeholders such as `{{order number}}` and `{{account type}}`. These appear in 893 tickets (17.9%).

A systematic investigation was conducted before deciding on a handling strategy:

All 9 placeholder types were identified and quantified:

| Placeholder | Occurrences |
|---|---|
| `{{order number}}` | 2,907 |
| `{{account type}}` | 1,011 |
| `{{person name}}` | 887 |
| `{{account category}}` | 822 |
| `{{refund amount}}` | 624 |
| `{{currency symbol}}` | 372 |
| `{{delivery city}}` | 234 |
| `{{delivery country}}` | 177 |
| `{{invoice number}}` | 8 |

The impact on specific intents was analysed. `{{order number}}` appeared in 97%+ of tickets for cancel_order, track_order and change_order:

| Intent | Total | With `{{order number}}` |
|---|---|---|
| cancel_order | 998 | 973 (97.5%) |
| change_order | 997 | 965 (96.8%) |
| track_order | 995 | 969 (97.4%) |

Dropping rows with placeholders entirely reduced these intents to 25-32 tickets — this approach was ruled out.

Three preprocessing approaches were evaluated empirically:

| Approach | Description | Silhouette | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Replace all with tokens e.g. `ORDER_NUMBER` | 0.828 | MEDIUM |
| 2 | Remove all placeholders entirely | 0.825 | MEDIUM |
| 3 | Targeted removal — remove meaningless, keep categorical | 0.831 | LOW |

All three produced statistically equivalent silhouette scores — the difference of <0.006 is within random variation from UMAP's stochastic nature. Approach 3 showed a nominally higher silhouette but LOW confidence due to a noisy silhouette plateau rather than a clean peak.

**Approach 2 selected.** Sentence transformers are trained on natural language. Placeholder tokens are out-of-vocabulary and produce unreliable subword embeddings. Removing them produces cleaner input for the embedding model.

### Embeddings

`all-MiniLM-L6-v2` — produces 384-dimensional sentence embeddings. `all-mpnet-base-v2` (768d) was tested empirically and showed no meaningful improvement for short customer support tickets (avg 8.5 words, silhouette difference of 0.004, within random variation). All embeddings L2 normalised before clustering.

### UMAP

Raw 384-dimensional embeddings produced a maximum silhouette of 0.19 with no peak — clustering was unreliable. UMAP-reduced embeddings produced silhouette of 0.889 with a clean peak. This was the most impactful technical decision in the project.

In 384 dimensions, distances between points become compressed and similar — the curse of dimensionality makes density-based distance calculations unreliable. UMAP removes noise dimensions while preserving neighbourhood structure.

A grid search was run across `n_neighbors ∈ {10, 15, 20, 30}` × `n_components ∈ {30, 50, 75}` for each algorithm independently, as K-Means and DBSCAN require different neighbourhood structures to perform optimally.

| | n_neighbors | n_components | min_dist | metric |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| K-Means UMAP | 30 | 75 | 0.0 | cosine |
| DBSCAN UMAP | 10 | 50 | 0.0 | cosine |
| Visualisation UMAP | 15 | 2 | 0.1 | cosine |

`min_dist=0.0` for clustering produces tight, compact clusters. `min_dist=0.1` for visualisation spreads points for readability.

### K-Means

Optimal k determined via silhouette score scanning (k=2 to 49) with kneedle elbow detection as a secondary signal. A confidence scoring system handles disagreement between the two methods:

| Confidence | Condition |
|---|---|
| High | Elbow and silhouette agree within ±3 |
| Medium | Inertia still dropping >30% at elbow — trust silhouette |
| Low | Strong disagreement — human review flagged |

Kneedle detected k=18 while silhouette peaked at k=22. Inertia was still dropping 64% between k=18 and k=22 — the confidence system correctly identified kneedle as having undershot on the gradual curve and trusted silhouette.

**Final model: k=22, n_init=10**

### DBSCAN

A grid search was run across 8 UMAP configurations and `eps ∈ {0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7}` with `min_samples=5`. The rule-of-thumb `min_samples = 2 × n_components` produced 100% noise at all eps values — customer support embeddings are too dense for this heuristic.

DBSCAN consistently found 44-85 clusters minimum regardless of parameters. This reflects the data structure — the UMAP space contains many tight micro-clusters that DBSCAN correctly identifies as separate density regions. Forcing fewer clusters would require eps values large enough to merge genuinely different topics.

**Final model: eps=0.7, min_samples=5, 69 clusters**

### Algorithm comparison

| Metric | K-Means | DBSCAN | Winner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Silhouette | **0.889** | 0.806 | K-Means |
| Davies-Bouldin | **0.151** | 0.243 | K-Means |
| ARI | 0.585 | **0.594** | DBSCAN |
| NMI | **0.863** | 0.823 | K-Means |
| N clusters | **22** | 69 | K-Means |
| Noise points | **0** | 0 | Tie |

**K-Means selected for production** — better on 3 of 4 metrics, 22 actionable topic groups vs 69 micro-clusters. DBSCAN marginally wins on ARI (+0.009) but at the cost of interpretability. The two algorithms answer different questions: K-Means finds macro topic structure, DBSCAN finds micro density boundaries.

### Prediction

New tickets are assigned to clusters via:
1. Sentence embedding with `all-MiniLM-L6-v2`
2. L2 normalisation
3. UMAP transform using saved reducer
4. K-Means predict using saved model

DBSCAN does not support prediction for new points — density-based algorithms have no centroid for assignment.

**macOS note:** A segmentation fault occurs when PyTorch and numba are loaded in the same process. Fixed by warming up numba JIT compilation before importing sentence-transformers. See `src/predict.py` for implementation.

## Key findings

- Raw embeddings (384d) produced silhouette of 0.19 — clustering only became viable after UMAP reduction
- Unsupervised clustering recovered 86.3% of true intent structure (NMI=0.863) without access to any labels during training
- Three placeholder preprocessing approaches were empirically equivalent — logical consistency drove the final decision over marginal metric differences
- Account-related intents merged into one large cluster due to shared vocabulary — a known limitation of general-purpose embeddings for fine-grained intent separation
- DBSCAN finds micro-structure (69 clusters), K-Means finds macro-structure (22 clusters) — neither is incorrect, they operate at different granularities
